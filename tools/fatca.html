FATCA

Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act

FATCA is US legislation (IRC Chapter 4) that requires Foreign Financial Institutions and certain non-financial entities to report information about financial accounts held by US Persons to the IRS — either directly or through their local tax authority under an Intergovernmental Agreement. Non-compliant institutions face a 30% withholding tax on applicable US-source payments.

Key difference from CRS

CRS reports on account holders who are tax resident in a participating jurisdiction. FATCA reports on US Persons — US citizens and US residents — regardless of where they live. A US citizen living in Norway is reportable under FATCA to the IRS, but not under CRS unless they are also tax resident in a CRS reporting jurisdiction.

IGA Implementation Models
Model 1 IGA Reporting FFIs report US account information to their local competent authority, which then automatically exchanges data with the IRS. Most jurisdictions — including EU member states — have adopted Model 1.
Model 2 IGA FFIs report US account information directly to the IRS under an FFI Agreement, with the local jurisdiction removing legal barriers to direct reporting. Less common than Model 1.

Reporting Timeline

FATCA follows an annual cycle. Deadlines vary depending on whether your jurisdiction operates under a Model 1 or Model 2 IGA, or whether you report directly to the IRS.

1
Collect

Throughout the year

Collect W-8/W-9 self-certifications for new accounts. Apply due diligence to pre-existing accounts. Monitor for changes in circumstances indicating US person status.

2
Review

By 31 December

Complete annual enhanced review of all High Value individual accounts (≥ USD 1,000,000). Confirm year-end balances and income for all reportable US accounts.

3
Report

Typically by 31 May / 30 June

Model 1: report to your local competent authority. Model 2 / direct: file Form 8966 with the IRS. Covers reportable US accounts as at 31 December of the prior year.

4
Exchange

Typically by 30 September

Model 1: your competent authority exchanges the reported data with the IRS. Model 2 / direct reporters have already submitted directly to the IRS in the prior step.

Due Diligence Thresholds

FATCA thresholds differ from CRS. Note in particular the lower individual account exemption threshold ($50,000 vs no exemption in CRS) and the specific cash value insurance threshold.

Individual Accounts
New account W-8/W-9 required — de minimis applies to certain accounts ≤ $50,000
Pre-existing — exempt Balance ≤ $50,000 (depository); ≤ $250,000 (cash value insurance / annuity)
Pre-existing — Lower Value $50,001 – $1,000,000 — electronic records search
Pre-existing — High Value Balance ≥ $1,000,000 — enhanced due diligence + relationship manager enquiry
Entity Accounts
New account W-8 series / W-9 required — no balance threshold
Pre-existing — exempt Balance ≤ USD 250,000 — no due diligence required
Pre-existing — above threshold Balance > USD 250,000 — full entity due diligence required

A Substantial US Owner is any US Person owning, directly or indirectly, more than 25% of a Passive NFFE. The following information must be reported for each Substantial US Owner alongside the entity account information.

Per Substantial US Owner
Full name
Address
US TIN (SSN, ITIN, or EIN)
Ownership percentage (direct and indirect combined)

Unlike CRS Controlling Persons (who are identified by residence), Substantial US Owners are identified by US citizenship or residency status. A 25% ownership threshold applies under FATCA, compared to the lower thresholds sometimes used in CRS.

Key Definitions

Select a term to expand its definition under FATCA.

Foreign Financial Institution (FFI)
Any non-US financial institution — including foreign banks, brokers, investment funds, and certain insurance companies — that accepts deposits, holds financial assets for others, invests in financial assets, or issues cash-value insurance or annuity contracts. FFIs are required to identify and report US account holders to the IRS, either directly or through their local tax authority under an IGA.
Participating FFI (PFFI)
An FFI that has entered into an FFI Agreement with the IRS and has been assigned a Global Intermediary Identification Number (GIIN). PFFIs in non-IGA jurisdictions report US account information directly to the IRS via Form 8966. In Model 2 IGA jurisdictions, FFIs report under the IGA framework but still maintain a GIIN.
Non-Participating FFI (NPFFI)
An FFI that has not entered into an FFI Agreement with the IRS and is not otherwise compliant with FATCA. Payments of US-source income (including certain gross proceeds) made to NPFFIs are subject to a 30% FATCA withholding tax. Being classified as an NPFFI creates significant reputational and financial risks for an institution.
Intergovernmental Agreement (IGA)
A bilateral agreement between the United States and a foreign government designed to facilitate FATCA compliance while addressing local legal barriers to direct reporting. Under a Model 1 IGA, FFIs report to their local competent authority which then exchanges data with the IRS. Under a Model 2 IGA, FFIs report directly to the IRS with their government's support.
US Person
For FATCA purposes, a US Person includes: (1) a US citizen (including dual citizens, regardless of country of residence); (2) a US lawful permanent resident (green card holder); (3) an individual meeting the substantial presence test (present in the US for at least 31 days in the current year and 183 days over the prior three years using a weighted formula); (4) a corporation, partnership, trust, or estate organised under US law.
Global Intermediary Identification Number (GIIN)
A 19-character identification number assigned by the IRS to FFIs that register on the FATCA Registration Portal. The GIIN identifies the FFI on Form W-8 series documentation and in FATCA reporting. Withholding agents use GIINs to verify FFI status on the IRS FFI List before making payments. A valid GIIN is required for Participating and Registered Deemed-Compliant FFIs.
Non-Financial Foreign Entity (NFFE)
Any foreign entity that is not a Financial Institution. NFFEs are classified as Active or Passive. An FFI must determine whether entity account holders are Active NFFEs, Passive NFFEs, or Financial Institutions in order to apply the correct due diligence and reporting procedures. NFFEs are not directly subject to FATCA reporting obligations, but their accounts at FFIs may be.
Active NFFE
An NFFE where less than 50% of its gross income for the preceding year is passive income AND less than 50% of its assets are held for the production of passive income. Active NFFEs also include publicly traded entities and their affiliates, start-up companies, and certain holding companies. Active NFFEs are not required to disclose Substantial US Owners.
Passive NFFE
Any NFFE that is not an Active NFFE. This includes holding companies, investment vehicles, and entities whose income is primarily passive (dividends, interest, royalties, rent). The FFI maintaining an account for a Passive NFFE must identify and report any Substantial US Owners — those individuals who own more than 25% of the entity directly or indirectly.
Substantial US Owner
Any US Person who directly or indirectly owns more than 25% of a Passive NFFE — measured by vote, value, or beneficial interest. For trusts, a Substantial US Owner includes any US Person who is treated as the owner of any portion of the trust under the grantor trust rules. Unlike CRS Controlling Persons, Substantial US Owners are determined by US person status rather than by jurisdiction of residence.
W-8 / W-9 Forms
The US IRS form series used by FFIs to collect account holder certifications under FATCA. Form W-9 is provided by US Persons certifying their US taxpayer status and TIN. Form W-8BEN is provided by foreign individuals certifying non-US status. Form W-8BEN-E is used by foreign entities. Other W-8 variants apply to specific entity types. The FFI must verify the reasonableness of these certifications against information on file.
US Indicia
Indicators in account records that suggest an account holder may be a US Person. Under FATCA, US indicia include: identification as a US citizen or US resident alien; a US place of birth; a current US mailing or residence address; a current US telephone number; standing instructions to transfer funds to a US account; a currently effective power of attorney granted to a person with a US address; and an "in care of" or hold mail address in the US as the sole address on file.
